Backup and Recovery

Intermediate 🇫🇷 Français

Why Back Up Your Data?

Imagine all the files in your office disappearing overnight — client contracts, financial reports, ongoing projects. A backup is your safety net: a copy of your data stored separately, ready to be restored when something goes wrong.

Real-world analogy: Backups work like photocopies of your important documents kept in a safe at the bank. If a fire destroys your office, you can rebuild your files from the copies stored in a secure location.

Incidents that can cause data loss are more common than you might think:

  • Human error — accidentally deleting an important file
  • Hardware failure — a hard drive or server breaking down
  • Cyberattack — ransomware encrypting your data
  • Natural disaster — flooding or fire affecting your premises

Essential Terms

  • Full backup — a complete copy of all your data. Like photocopying every single file in your cabinets.
  • Incremental backup — a copy of only the data that has changed since the last backup. Like photocopying only the new pages that were added.
  • Restore point — a specific moment in time you can return to. Like a bookmark in time that marks the state of your data at a given moment.
  • RPO (Recovery Point Objective) — the maximum amount of data you can accept losing, measured in time. An RPO of 1 hour means you could lose at most 1 hour of work.
  • RTO (Recovery Time Objective) — the maximum acceptable time to restore your data after an incident. An RTO of 4 hours means your systems must be operational within 4 hours.

The Backup Process

The backup process in CloudStore follows several automated steps. Once configured, it runs without manual intervention.

What happens at each step

  • Scheduling — You define when backups should occur (e.g., every night at 2 AM).
  • Selection — The system identifies which data has changed since the last backup.
  • Transfer — Data is encrypted then sent to CloudStore via a secure channel.
  • Storage — The backup is replicated across multiple geographic locations for added safety.
  • Verification — CloudStore verifies the copy is intact and sends you a confirmation.

The Recovery Process

Recovery (or restoration) is the reverse process: retrieving your data from a backup when an incident occurs. It's like going to the bank's safe to collect your photocopies after a disaster.

What happens at each step

  • Detection — An incident is identified (deleted file, corrupted data, system failure).
  • Point selection — You choose which point in time you want to return to (e.g., "yesterday at 6 PM").
  • Extraction — CloudStore retrieves the corresponding backup from its secure locations.
  • Restoration — Data is placed back into your working environment.
  • Validation — You verify that the restored data is complete and functional.

Backup Best Practices

The 3-2-1 rule: Keep 3 copies of your data, on 2 different types of media, with 1 copy offsite. CloudStore makes following this rule easy by automatically providing offsite storage and redundancy.

Tips for customer conversations

  • Test regularly — A backup that has never been tested is not reliable. Recommend quarterly restoration tests.
  • Match frequency to change rate — The more often data changes, the more frequent backups should be.
  • Set priorities — Not all data is equally important. Critical data deserves more frequent backups.
  • Document the plan — In an emergency, everyone needs to know who does what and in what order.

Key Takeaways

  • Backup creates safety copies of your data to protect against loss.
  • Recovery restores your data from those copies when an incident occurs.
  • The backup process is automated: scheduling, selection, transfer, storage, and verification.
  • The recovery process is guided: detection, point selection, extraction, restoration, and validation.
  • Follow the 3-2-1 rule and test your restorations regularly.
