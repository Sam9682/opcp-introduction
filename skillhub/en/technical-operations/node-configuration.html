📖 Glossary

Node Configuration

Intermediate

Overview

Node configuration determines how your cloud resources behave and perform. Each configuration option directly affects what your customers experience — from how fast their applications respond to whether their service stays online during maintenance.

Real-world analogy: Configuring a node is like setting up a new retail store. You decide the opening hours (availability), how many checkout lanes to open (performance), whether to have a backup generator (resilience), and how to organise the stockroom (storage). Each decision shapes the customer experience.

Availability Configuration

Availability settings determine how reliably your service stays accessible to users.

High Availability

What it does: Runs your workload across multiple physical servers simultaneously, so if one server encounters a problem, another takes over instantly.

Customer impact: Enabling high availability means your service stays online even if one physical server fails. Your users experience no interruption — the switchover happens automatically in seconds.

Analogy: Like having two bridges crossing a river. If one is closed for repairs, traffic automatically flows over the other without drivers needing to find a detour.

When customers need this: Any service where downtime directly costs money or damages reputation — e-commerce sites, customer portals, real-time communication tools.

Maintenance Window Scheduling

What it does: Allows you to define when routine platform updates and maintenance activities occur.

Customer impact: Scheduling maintenance windows during off-peak hours means your users are less likely to notice any brief service adjustments. Business operations continue uninterrupted during critical hours.

Analogy: Like scheduling road repairs for 2 AM instead of rush hour — the work still gets done, but fewer people are affected.

When customers need this: Organisations with predictable usage patterns who want to minimise any potential impact on their busiest periods.

Performance Configuration

Performance settings control how quickly your applications respond and how much work they can handle simultaneously.

Resource Scaling

What it does: Adjusts the amount of processing power and memory allocated to your node, either manually or based on demand.

Customer impact: Proper resource scaling ensures your application responds quickly even during traffic spikes. Your customers never experience slowdowns during peak usage — the system grows to meet demand.

Analogy: Like a restaurant that can open additional dining rooms when a large party arrives, rather than making guests wait for a table.

When customers need this: Services with variable demand — seasonal businesses, event-driven traffic, or growing organisations whose needs change over time.

Storage Performance Tiers

What it does: Selects the speed at which data can be read from and written to storage, ranging from standard to high-performance tiers.

Customer impact: Choosing a higher storage performance tier means your applications load data faster. Database queries return results more quickly, file uploads complete sooner, and overall application responsiveness improves.

Analogy: Like choosing between a standard postal service and express delivery. Both get the package there, but express is significantly faster when speed matters.

When customers need this: Applications that frequently read or write large amounts of data — databases, media processing, analytics platforms.

Network Configuration

Network settings control how your node communicates with the outside world and with other resources in your environment.

Bandwidth Allocation

What it does: Sets the maximum amount of data that can flow to and from your node per second.

Customer impact: Adequate bandwidth allocation ensures your users can download files, stream content, and interact with your application without delays or buffering — even when many users are connected simultaneously.

Analogy: Like choosing the width of a water pipe. A wider pipe delivers more water at once, serving more taps without reducing pressure at any single one.

When customers need this: Services that deliver large files, stream media, or serve many concurrent users — content delivery, video platforms, large-scale web applications.

Network Isolation

What it does: Creates private network segments that separate your resources from other tenants and from the public internet.

Customer impact: Network isolation means your sensitive data travels only through private channels, never exposed to the public internet. This provides an additional layer of security and helps meet regulatory compliance requirements.

Analogy: Like having a private road between your office buildings rather than using public streets. Only your vehicles travel on it, and outsiders cannot access it.

When customers need this: Organisations handling sensitive data — healthcare, finance, government — or any business that needs to demonstrate strong security controls to auditors.

Cost-Related Configuration

Resource Limits

What it does: Sets maximum caps on resource consumption to prevent unexpected cost increases.

Customer impact: Setting resource limits means your monthly bill stays predictable. You will never be surprised by unexpected charges from a sudden spike in usage — the system respects the boundaries you define.

Analogy: Like setting a spending limit on a company credit card. The card works normally up to the limit, then stops to prevent overspending.

When customers need this: Budget-conscious organisations, departments with fixed IT allocations, or any customer who values cost predictability over unlimited flexibility.

Reserved vs. On-Demand Resources

What it does: Allows you to commit to a resource level for a longer period (reserved) at a lower rate, or pay a higher rate for flexibility (on-demand).

Customer impact: Choosing reserved resources reduces your per-unit cost significantly — typically 20-40% savings compared to on-demand pricing. Your capacity is guaranteed and always available when you need it.

Analogy: Like choosing between a monthly gym membership (reserved — cheaper per visit, committed) versus paying per session (on-demand — more expensive per visit, but no commitment).

When customers need this: Organisations with stable, predictable workloads benefit from reserved pricing. Those with unpredictable or temporary needs benefit from on-demand flexibility.

What to Tell Customers

  • Every configuration option has a direct, understandable impact on their service quality or cost
  • High availability protects against unexpected outages — their users stay connected
  • Resource scaling means they only pay for what they use, while always having enough capacity
  • Network isolation provides enterprise-grade security without complex setup
  • Resource limits prevent bill shock — costs stay within defined boundaries
  • Configuration changes can be made at any time through the management interface — they are not locked into initial choices
