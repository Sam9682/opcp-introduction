Organising Your Digital Data

Storing data is only the first step. To get the most out of it, you need to organise it, protect it, and know when to keep or delete it. Data management is like running a well-organised library: every book has its place, old volumes are archived, and important documents are kept safe.

Data management refers to the set of practices for collecting, storing, organising, maintaining, and using data effectively and securely throughout its existence. In CloudStore, these practices are built into the platform to help you manage your storage efficiently.

The Data Lifecycle

All data follows a journey from creation to deletion. Understanding this cycle helps you make the right decisions about storage and protection.

Essential Terms

Before going further, here are the key terms you'll encounter in data management:

  • Object — a unit of data stored in CloudStore (a file, an image, a document). Think of it as an individual book in a library.
  • Container (or bucket) — a logical grouping of objects, like a shelf in the library that gathers books on the same subject.
  • Metadata — information describing your data (creation date, size, type). It's the equivalent of the catalogue card attached to each book.
  • Retention policy — a rule defining how long data must be kept before deletion. Like the legal requirement to keep invoices for a certain number of years.
  • Versioning — the automatic preservation of previous versions of a file each time it's modified. Like keeping all drafts of a contract.

Organising Your Data

Good organisation makes it easier to find, share, and protect data. Here are the best practices:

Structure by project or department

Create separate containers for each project or department. It's like having distinct cabinets for accounting, marketing, and human resources — everyone knows where to look.

Consistent naming

Use clear and uniform naming conventions. For example: 2024-01-sales-report-Q1.pdf is much easier to find than doc_final_v2_FINAL.pdf.

Tagging

Add tags (labels) to your objects to categorise them. Tags are keywords you attach to files to find them easily, like coloured sticky notes on folders.

Retention Policies

Not all data needs to be kept forever. Retention policies help you:

  • Reduce costs — deleting obsolete data frees up space and reduces your bill.
  • Meet regulations — some laws require keeping data for a minimum period (e.g., 7 years for financial records in many jurisdictions).
  • Limit risk — less stored data means less data exposed in the event of a security incident.

Real-world analogy: Think of retention policies like the rules of a public library. Today's newspapers are freely available, last week's go to the reserve, those over a year old are archived in the basement, and those over 10 years old are recycled — except historic editions which are kept indefinitely.

File Versioning

Versioning automatically keeps a history of every change made to a file. If someone accidentally overwrites an important document, you can revert to a previous version in just a few clicks.

When to use versioning

  • Collaborative documents edited by multiple people
  • Critical configuration files
  • Contractual data requiring an audit trail

Key Takeaways

  • Data follows a lifecycle: creation, active storage, archiving, retention, and deletion.
  • Good organisation (containers, naming, tags) makes day-to-day management easier.
  • Retention policies reduce costs and ensure regulatory compliance.
  • Versioning protects against accidental changes by preserving file history.
  • CloudStore provides the tools to manage every stage of the lifecycle without technical expertise.
